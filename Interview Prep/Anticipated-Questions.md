## How I'd walk you through the framework
Top-down, so it's easy to follow:
1. **The README** — the whole training landscape, organized by lean value streams.
2. **The Trade & Progression Index** — every role mapped to its trade and apprenticeship pathway, on one page.
3. **One process in depth (e.g., Welding)** — training document → certifications → trade progression → a worked gap analysis. This is what depth looks like for every one of the 46 processes.
4. **The Program-Management loop** — the engine that keeps it current: Verify → Develop → Train → Assess → Document → Audit.
5. **The 90-day plan** — how I'd turn the framework into an operating program.

## Turning features into outcomes
Everything here maps to something the business cares about:
- Requirement-to-rule traceability → **audit-ready by design** (DCAA, AS9100, FAA, DCSA).
- Apprentice → Journeyman → Master pathways → **retention and a homegrown talent pipeline.**
- Lean value streams → **training effort focused where it adds value; quality built in.**
- Continuous-improvement loop + KPIs → **a measurable program, not a binder on a shelf.**
- Version control → **every change tracked, reviewable, and auditable.**

**Did you build this from any proprietary or internal information?**
> No — it's built entirely from public capability statements and publicly available standards.
> That was deliberate: I wanted to show my method without touching anything I shouldn't.

**Isn't it presumptuous to design our program before you know it?**
> It's a fair point, and it's why the framework is built around learning your current state
> first. The entire first phase is verifying real requirements and gap-analyzing against what
> you already do. I'm showing how I'd approach the work, not claiming your program is wrong — I
> expect a lot of this to change once I see your actual contracts and current practice.

**Did you use AI to build this?**
> Yes. I used AI tooling to accelerate the buildout. The structure, the lean approach, the
> compliance mapping, and the decisions are mine — the tool let me execute quickly. Being able
> to use modern tools to move fast and produce quality work is part of what I'd bring to the role.

**How is this different from an LMS or the system we already use?**
> This is the layer above an LMS — the program architecture and governance. It decides *what*
> training is required, *why*, *who* needs it, *how* we prove competency, and *how* we improve.
> An LMS delivers and tracks courses; this decides what belongs in the LMS and keeps it tied to
> compliance.

**What would you do in your first 90 days?**
> Days 0–30: learn the real state — pull contract training flow-downs, review current records,
> meet the process owners. Days 31–60: run the gap analysis per process and prioritize the
> highest compliance-risk gaps. Days 61–90: stand up the improvement loop and a metrics
> dashboard, and scope the first one or two DOL apprenticeship programs.

**Our current program already covers a lot of this — why change?**
> Then you're ahead, and my job is to strengthen and prove it, not replace it. The value I add
> is the connective tissue — traceability to requirements, competency verification, metrics, and
> continuous improvement — so what you already do stays defensible in an audit and keeps pace
> with new contracts.

**How do you measure success in this role?**
> Certification currency and training-completion rates, time-to-qualify, audit findings and how
> quickly we close them, apprentices advancing tiers, and retention / fill rates — on a simple
> dashboard leadership can read at a glance.

> initiative, systems thinking, and a compliance-first, continuous-improvement mindset. This

**How would you handle a subsidiary that resists the process?**
