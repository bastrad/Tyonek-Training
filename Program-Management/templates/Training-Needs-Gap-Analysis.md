> ## Part 1 — Read this first (plain-language primer)
>
> **What is this document?** It is a two-sided comparison. On one side is **what training
> the law and our customers require** for this job. On the other side is **what the company
> is actually doing today**. The difference between the two is a **"gap."** This document
> finds those gaps so we can fix them.
>
> **Two simple definitions:**
> - **Training need** = a piece of training that a rule says people in this job *must* have.
> - **Gap** = a training need that is *not yet being met* (missing, outdated, or unverified).
>
> **Why is the "current-state" side blank right now?** We are building this **from scratch**.
> We have listed everything that is *required* (Part 2), but we do **not yet have the data on
> what the company is currently doing**. That data has to be collected from the actual work
> site, training records, and managers (see the checklist in Part 3). Until then, the gap
> columns stay marked **"PENDING."** This is normal and correct — you cannot measure a gap
> until you know both sides.
>
> **How to use this document (3 steps):**
> 1. **Pick what applies.** In Part 2, select the rows that apply to *this* work process.
>    (Not every rule applies to every job — a welder and a cyber analyst have different rules.)
> 2. **Collect current-state data.** Use the Part 3 checklist to gather what the company
>    does now.
> 3. **Fill the worksheet and find the gaps.** Compare required vs. current, mark each row,
>    and route every gap to an action (train, develop new material, or fix records).
>
> **How to read a citation:** e.g. `29 CFR 1910.147` means *Title 29 of the Code of Federal
> Regulations, Part 1910, Section 147* — the government rulebook, chapter and verse. You do
> not need to memorize these; they are there so anyone can look up the exact rule.
>
> **Mini-glossary (plain terms):**
> | Term | Plain meaning |
> |---|---|
> | **Required** | The law or the contract says you *must* have it. Not optional. |
> | **Recommended** | Strongly advised; improves quality/compliance but not legally forced. |
> | **Nice-to-have** | Adds skill or growth; not needed to do the job safely/legally. |
> | **Initial training** | The first-time training before someone does the task. |
> | **Recurrent / refresher** | Repeat training on a schedule (e.g., every year) to stay current. |
> | **Competence** | Proof a person can actually *do* the task correctly — not just attend a class. |
> | **OJT** | On-the-Job Training — learning by doing the real work under supervision. |
> | **SME** | Subject-Matter Expert — the experienced person who knows the job. |

## Part 2 — The Governing Material (what *requires* training)

These are the outside rules that **force** training. They come from four kinds of authority:
**safety law**, **environmental law**, **quality standards**, and **contract/customer/security
mandates**. Because Tyonek is a **government contractor**, anything a regulation, statute, or
contract flows down is **Required**.

Select the categories that apply to **[Subsidiary] / [Work Process]** using the "Applies to"
column. (Sector tags: **MFG** = Manufacturing, **MRO** = Aviation Services, **MS** = Mission
Support, **CON** = Construction, **ALL** = every subsidiary.)

### A. Workplace Safety — OSHA
The master reference is **OSHA Publication 2254, "Training Requirements in OSHA Standards."**
OSHA does not have one "safety class"; instead, *each hazard* has its own training rule.

| Governing source | What it requires (plain) | Applies to | Training type & frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hazard Communication — 29 CFR 1910.1200 | Know the chemicals you work with (labels, safety data sheets) | ALL | Initial + when a new hazard is introduced |
| Lockout/Tagout — 29 CFR 1910.147 | Safely shut off machine energy before service | MFG, MRO, CON | Initial + retrain on change/deficiency |
| PPE — 29 CFR 1910.132 | Correct use of protective equipment | ALL | Initial + when equipment/hazard changes |
| Respiratory Protection — 29 CFR 1910.134 | Proper respirator use + medical clearance | MFG (paint/weld), MRO | Initial + **annual** fit test/training |
| Hearing Conservation — 29 CFR 1910.95 | Protect hearing in high-noise areas | MFG, MRO | **Annual** where noise exceeds limits |
| Hexavalent Chromium / Lead — 1910.1026 / 1910.1025 | Handle weld fume / lead exposure safely | MFG (weld, coatings) | Initial + as exposure conditions require |
| Respirable Silica — 29 CFR 1910.1053 | Control silica dust (abrasive/cutting) | MFG | Initial + as tasks require |
| Powered Industrial Trucks (forklift) — 1910.178 | Certified to operate forklifts | MFG, MRO, CON | Initial + **evaluation every 3 years** |
| Confined Space — 1910.146 / 1926 Subpart AA | Safe entry into tanks/booths/vaults | MFG, MRO, CON | Initial + on change |
| HAZWOPER — 29 CFR 1910.120 | Hazardous-material/emergency response | MRO, CON (as applicable) | 24–40 hr initial + **8 hr annual** refresher |
| Fire Extinguishers / Emergency Action Plan — 1910.157 / .38 | Respond to fire/evacuation | ALL | Initial + **annual** |
| Welding, Cutting & Hot Work — 1910.252 | Safe hot-work practices/fire watch | MFG, MRO | Initial + as tasks require |
| Electrical Safety-Related Work — 1910.331–.335 | Safe work around energized systems | MFG, MRO, MS | Initial + as tasks require |
| Bloodborne Pathogens — 1910.1030 | First-aid/exposure control (if responders) | ALL (first-aid teams) | Initial + **annual** |
